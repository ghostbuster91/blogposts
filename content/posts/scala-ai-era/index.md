*Reflections after Scalar*

There's a narrative that Scala has been losing momentum for years.

After attending Scalar, I'm starting to think the opposite might be happening.

### A Language with Slow-Building Momentum

Scala has always been... different.

It attracts a certain kind of developer:

* curious
* ambitious
* willing to go deeper than average

I don't know if it's the language features, the type system, or the kinds of problems people choose to solve with it - but somehow, Scala tends to attract very strong engineers.

And yet, despite that, its momentum has always felt... slow.

Why?

Because the language is extremely flexible.
Because there are many ways to do the same thing.
Because the ecosystem is fragmented.

Instead of one clear direction, there were many.

That meant the "snowball effect" (the compounding growth of libraries, tools, and community) never accelerated as fast as in more opinionated ecosystems.


### The Real Bottleneck Was Never Talent

Listening to recent discussions, including Łukasz Biały's talk, there's a strong argument that Scala is a great language for AI-assisted development.

Mainly because:

> the compiler helps you a lot.

And that’s true.

But I think there's another dimension that deserves more attention.

The bottleneck wasn't a lack of talent - it was how much output that talent could realistically produce.

Scala already has:

* incredibly talented developers
* strong ideas
* ambitious projects

What it lacked was **throughput**.


### The Throughput Problem

Before AI, we were limited by a very simple constraint:

> how fast we can turn ideas into code

Not just typing speed - that's irrelevant.

It's about:

* navigating codebases
* writing boilerplate
* stitching pieces together
* maintaining focus over long stretches of repetitive work

There's always a point in a project where:

* the idea is validated
* the architecture makes sense
* everything "clicks"

...and then you hit the wall.

Too much boilerplate.
Too much effort.
Not enough time.

And the project dies.

---

### AI Removes the Friction

With AI, that constraint largely disappears.

You can:

* generate boilerplate instantly
* explore multiple ideas in parallel
* iterate much faster
* keep the creative momentum alive

This changes everything.

Now what matters is:

> having a vision and pushing it forward

Not grinding through implementation details.


### What Happens When Strong Engineers Are Unblocked

We're already starting to see early signals of this shift.

Łukasz Biały recently described how he [forked](https://github.com/lbialy/parlance) the [Magnum](https://github.com/AugustNagro/magnum) project and, within roughly a month, turned it into a fully-fledged ORM - effectively writing as much code as the original author in that time.

Or take Mateusz Kubuszok, who built a compatibility layer between Scala 2 and Scala 3 macros - not just a thin bridge, but a unified abstraction that actually simplifies building certain patterns. To prove it works, he created [Kindlings](https://github.com/kubuszok/kindlings), porting a number of existing libraries onto this new foundation.

These are not small projects.
They are the kind of efforts that previously required:

* long timelines
* sustained motivation
* significant manual effort

Now they are happening faster - and more often.


### Why This Matters for Scala

This is where Scala becomes uniquely interesting.

Because now:

* the *cost of complexity* drops
* but the *value of strong abstractions* remains

And Scala is built exactly for that:

* expressive modeling
* powerful type system
* rich ecosystem foundations

Most importantly:

* it already has a community full of people who know how to build interesting things

The only thing that was missing was speed.

### 10x Developers Become 100x

Here's the intuition I can't shake:

> In the AI era, a small group of very strong Scala developers can produce the output of entire teams in other ecosystems.

Not because Scala is magically better.

But because:

* the people are strong
* the ideas are ambitious
* and now the execution speed is finally there

The constraint has shifted.

### A Delayed Payoff?

Which leads to a more provocative question:

> Were all those years of focusing on correctness, type safety, and quality, at the cost of slower development, actually an investment that is only now starting to pay off?

Scala has always optimized for:

* correctness
* composability
* abstraction

But those came with a cost:

* longer development cycles
* higher entry barriers
* slower ecosystem growth

If AI removes the cost, but preserves the benefits...

then we might be entering a phase where:

> the trade-offs finally flip in Scala’s favor.


### A Different Kind of Momentum

If this holds, Scala doesn't need to compete head-on with mainstream languages.

It can grow differently:

* not through mass adoption
* but through high-leverage output

Fewer developers.
More impact.

And that might finally create the momentum that was missing for years.

---

### Looking Ahead

My bet is simple:

> In the next year or two, we'll start seeing a wave of new Scala projects that simply wouldn't have existed before AI.

Not because the ideas are new.

But because now they can actually be finished.

---

### Final Thought

Scala didn't suddenly become better.

The environment around it changed.

And that might be exactly what it needed.
